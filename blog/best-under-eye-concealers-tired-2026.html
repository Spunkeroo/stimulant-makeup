Understanding Your Dark Circles

Not all dark circles are created equal, and the right concealer depends on what is causing yours. There are three primary types of dark circles, each requiring a different approach to concealment and treatment.

Vascular dark circles (blue-purple). These are the most common type and are caused by dilated blood vessels visible through the thin under-eye skin. The skin around your eyes is only 0.5mm thick, roughly four times thinner than the rest of your face. When blood vessels dilate from fatigue, allergies, or genetics, the blue-purple color shows through. These respond best to peach or salmon color correctors underneath concealer, and caffeine eye creams can reduce their intensity before makeup application.

Pigmented dark circles (brown). Common in deeper skin tones and those with Mediterranean, South Asian, or African heritage, these are caused by excess melanin production around the eyes. Sun exposure, genetics, and post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation contribute to this type. They require brightening concealers with vitamin C or niacinamide and orange-toned color correctors for medium to dark skin.

Structural dark circles (shadows). These result from hollowed tear troughs, loss of under-eye fat pad volume, or prominent orbital bones creating shadows. No amount of concealer fully corrects structural shadows because the issue is three-dimensional. Light-reflecting concealers help, but managing expectations is important. For significant structural hollowing, dermal fillers are the most effective solution, while concealer provides partial camouflage.

Color Correcting Before Concealer

Color correcting uses complementary colors on the color wheel to neutralize discoloration before applying concealer. This technique is essential for moderate to severe dark circles that concealer alone cannot cover.

For light to medium skin with blue-purple circles: Use a peach or salmon color corrector. The peach tone neutralizes the blue-purple without looking ashy. Apply sparingly to the darkest areas only, blend, then layer concealer on top. L.A. Girl Pro Conceal in Peach ($5) and Bobbi Brown Corrector in Light Peach ($30) are excellent options at different price points.

For medium to dark skin with dark circles: Use an orange or deep peach corrector. The deeper tones are necessary to counteract darkness against medium-to-dark skin. NYX Professional Makeup HD Photogenic Concealer in Orange ($6) and Pixi Correction Concentrate in Brightening Peach ($14) both work well.

For deep skin with dark circles: Use a red or deep orange corrector. The depth of color needs to match the depth of your skin tone for effective correction. Black Opal Total Coverage Concealing Foundation in Beautiful Bronze and similar deep-toned correctors provide the intensity needed.

Application rule: Less is more with color correctors. Apply only to the discolored areas, not the entire under-eye. Blend thoroughly before layering concealer on top. The corrector should be fully blended and nearly invisible before concealer application. If you can see the corrector color, you have applied too much or not blended sufficiently.

Application Technique for Tired Eyes

Even the best concealer fails with poor technique. Here is the method professional makeup artists use to maximize the tired-eye-hiding power of any concealer.

Step 1: Prep the under-eye area. Apply a caffeine eye cream (like The Ordinary Caffeine Solution 5%) and wait 2 minutes for absorption. The caffeine de-puffs the area while the moisturizing base creates a smooth surface for concealer. If you skip this step, concealer clings to dry patches and settles into fine lines within hours.

Step 2: Apply concealer in an inverted triangle. Rather than tracing the shape of your dark circle (which only highlights it), apply concealer in an inverted triangle from the inner corner of the eye, down to the mid-cheek, and back up to the outer corner. This shape catches light, lifts the midface, and provides a natural brightening effect that blending along the dark circle alone cannot achieve.

Step 3: Blend with the right tool. Use a slightly damp beauty sponge (like the Beautyblender) and bounce — never drag — the product into the skin. The bouncing motion pushes product into the skin for a seamless finish. Dragging moves the concealer around and creates streaks. Alternatively, use your ring finger with gentle tapping motions. The ring finger applies the least pressure of any finger, which is ideal for the delicate eye area.

Step 4: Set strategically. Lightly press a small amount of finely milled translucent powder onto the concealer using a small, fluffy brush or a damp sponge. Press, do not sweep. Apply powder only to the areas where creasing occurs — typically the inner corner and directly beneath the eye. Skip the outer corner to maintain a natural, luminous finish.

How to Set Concealer Without Creasing

Creasing is the most common concealer complaint, and it is almost always caused by one of three things: too much product, wrong formula, or incorrect setting technique.

Use less product. The number one cause of creasing is over-application. Two thin layers blend better and crease less than one thick layer. If you need more coverage, let the first thin layer set for 60 seconds, then apply a second thin layer only where needed.

Choose the right powder. Not all setting powders are created equal. Laura Mercier Translucent Loose Setting Powder is the industry standard because its finely milled particles set without adding visible texture. Avoid powders with large particles or visible shimmer, as these exaggerate fine lines. For extremely dry under-eyes, try a setting spray instead of powder.

The baking alternative. Baking (applying a thick layer of powder, leaving it for 5 to 10 minutes, then dusting off the excess) creates a crease-proof under-eye that lasts all day. However, baking looks heavy on mature skin and in natural light. Reserve this technique for photography, events, or when you need makeup to last 12 or more hours without touch-ups.

Mistakes That Make Tired Eyes Worse

  • Choosing the wrong shade. Concealer that matches your exact skin tone does nothing for dark circles. You need one to two shades lighter to brighten. Conversely, going three or more shades lighter creates an obvious white ring that draws attention to the under-eye area rather than away from it.
  • Applying concealer before foundation. Foundation often provides enough coverage over the under-eye area that you need less concealer than expected. Apply foundation first, assess coverage, then use concealer only where additional brightening is needed. This prevents over-application.
  • Using matte concealer on dry skin. Matte formulas cling to every dry patch and fine line, making tired eyes look worse. If your under-eye skin is dry (test by smiling and looking for crepe-like texture), choose a radiant or satin-finish formula exclusively.
  • Skipping eye cream. Applying concealer to unprepped, dehydrated under-eye skin guarantees creasing and caking by midday. Two minutes of eye cream before concealer dramatically improves wear time and finish quality.
  • Over-powdering. Heavy powder under the eyes adds years. Use the minimum amount necessary to prevent creasing, and apply only to crease-prone areas, not the entire under-eye zone.

Frequently Asked Questions

What type of concealer is best for dark circles?
For blue-purple dark circles, choose a peach or salmon-toned color corrector underneath a concealer one shade lighter than your skin tone. For brown dark circles caused by hyperpigmentation, choose a concealer with brightening ingredients like vitamin C or niacinamide. Full coverage liquid concealers like Nars Radiant Creamy Concealer work best for moderate to severe dark circles.
Should under eye concealer be lighter than foundation?
Yes, typically one to two shades lighter than your skin tone or foundation. This brightening effect counteracts the shadows that create the appearance of dark circles. However, going more than two shades lighter creates an obvious contrast that looks unnatural. Choose one shade lighter for subtle brightening, two shades for more dramatic correction.
How do I stop concealer from creasing under my eyes?
Apply a thin layer rather than building up heavy coverage. Hydrate the under-eye area with eye cream first and let it absorb for 2 minutes. Set with a minimal amount of finely milled translucent powder using a pressing motion, not a sweeping motion. Avoid powders with large particles. Use a hydrating concealer formula specifically designed for the eye area.
What is the best concealer for mature skin under eyes?
Mature skin needs hydrating, medium-coverage formulas that do not settle into fine lines. The best options are Bobbi Brown Skin Full Cover Concealer, NARS Radiant Creamy Concealer, and IT Cosmetics Bye Bye Under Eye. Avoid matte concealers as they accentuate lines. Look for ingredients like hyaluronic acid, peptides, and caffeine in the formula.
Is color corrector necessary before concealer?
Color corrector is only necessary for moderate to severe dark circles that concealer alone cannot cover. For mild dark circles, a brightening concealer one shade lighter is sufficient. If you can still see darkness through one layer of concealer, add a color corrector underneath. Peach corrects blue-purple circles on light-medium skin. Orange corrects on medium-dark skin. Red corrects on deep skin tones.
