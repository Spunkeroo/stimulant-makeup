Best Cruelty-Free Makeup Brands

Cruelty-Free Certifications Explained

The term "cruelty-free" is not regulated by the FDA or any government agency in the United States. This means any brand can slap a bunny logo on their packaging and claim cruelty-free status without any verification. This is why third-party certifications are essential for consumers who want genuine assurance that no animals were harmed.

Leaping Bunny (Gold Standard)

The Leaping Bunny Program, administered by the Coalition for Consumer Information on Cosmetics (CCIC), is the most rigorous cruelty-free certification available. To earn the Leaping Bunny logo, a company must implement a supplier monitoring system that ensures no animal testing is conducted by the company, its laboratories, or its ingredient suppliers at any stage of product development. Companies must recommit annually and are subject to independent audits. The Leaping Bunny standard goes beyond the finished product to verify the entire supply chain, which is what distinguishes it from less rigorous certifications.

PETA Beauty Without Bunnies

PETA maintains two lists: companies that do not test on animals, and companies that are both cruelty-free and vegan. To be listed, companies must sign a statement of assurance verifying that they do not conduct, commission, or pay for animal testing. While less rigorous than Leaping Bunny (PETA does not conduct independent audits), PETA certification is widely recognized and covers a larger number of brands. The PETA database is searchable online and is a useful starting point for identifying cruelty-free options.

Choose Cruelty Free (CCF)

The CCF program, based in Australia, requires companies to sign a statutory declaration that neither they nor their ingredient suppliers test on animals. CCF also requires that the company not be owned by a parent company that tests on animals. This additional criterion makes CCF one of the strictest programs, as it excludes brands like CoverGirl (owned by Coty, which has tested on animals) even if the brand itself does not test.

Cruelty-Free vs Vegan: The Difference

These terms are frequently confused but address different concerns. Cruelty-free refers exclusively to testing practices: no animals were used to test the product or its ingredients. Vegan refers to formulation: no animal-derived ingredients are present in the product. A cruelty-free product can contain animal ingredients. Common animal-derived ingredients in makeup include beeswax (a thickener and emollient), carmine (a red pigment made from crushed cochineal beetles), lanolin (a moisturizer derived from sheep's wool oil), collagen (from animal connective tissue), guanine (from fish scales, used for shimmer), and squalene from shark liver (though plant-derived squalane from olives is increasingly common).

For consumers who want both ethical testing and ethical ingredients, look for products labeled both cruelty-free and vegan, or look for vegan certifications from organizations like the Vegan Society. Many brands in this guide offer entirely vegan product lines, while others have a mix of vegan and non-vegan products within their range.

Top 10 Cruelty-Free Makeup Brands Ranked

1. Fenty Beauty

Best Overall
Fenty Beauty by Rihanna
Fenty Beauty revolutionized the industry when it launched in 2017 with 40 foundation shades, proving that inclusivity and performance go hand in hand. Now offering 50+ shades in their Pro Filt'r foundation line. Leaping Bunny certified. Not 100% vegan (some products contain beeswax and carmine) but the majority of the line is vegan. Standout products: Pro Filt'r Soft Matte Foundation ($40), Gloss Bomb Universal Lip Luminizer ($22), and Killawatt Freestyle Highlighter ($38). The formulations consistently rank among the best in independent testing for coverage, longevity, and finish. Available at Sephora and fentybeauty.com.

2. e.l.f. Cosmetics

Best Drugstore
e.l.f. Cosmetics
e.l.f. (Eyes Lips Face) is 100% cruelty-free, 100% vegan, and Leaping Bunny certified. The entire brand is free from phthalates, parabens, nonylphenol ethoxylates, triclosan, triclocarban, and hydroquinone. What makes e.l.f. exceptional is the price point: most products are under $10, with many under $5. Standout products: Camo CC Cream ($15), Putty Blush ($7), Halo Glow Liquid Filter ($14), and Power Grip Primer ($10). The Halo Glow was a viral sensation as a dupe for Charlotte Tilbury's Flawless Filter at a fraction of the price. Available everywhere from Target to Ulta to Walmart.

Best Drugstore Cruelty-Free Brands

You do not need to spend $40 on foundation to avoid animal testing. The drugstore cruelty-free market has exploded, with brands like e.l.f., NYX, Milani, Wet n Wild, and Pacifica offering complete product lines at accessible prices.

e.l.f. is the standout at the drugstore level. Everything is under $15, most products are under $8, and the quality consistently rivals mid-range brands. The Halo Glow Liquid Filter, Camo CC Cream, and Power Grip Primer have all gone viral for matching the performance of products costing 3-4 times as much. e.l.f. proves that ethical beauty does not require a luxury budget.

Wet n Wild deserves mention for being both Leaping Bunny certified and incredibly affordable, with most products under $6. Their MegaGlo Highlighting Powder and Photo Focus Foundation are considered some of the best drugstore products available regardless of cruelty-free status.

Pacifica is 100% vegan, cruelty-free, and uses clean ingredients. Their products are available at Target and Ulta, with prices mostly between $10-$18. The Alight Clean Foundation and Cosmic Reflect Liquid Highlight are standouts.

Best Luxury Cruelty-Free Brands

The luxury cruelty-free space has grown significantly. Charlotte Tilbury's Leaping Bunny certification in 2021 was a major milestone. Other notable luxury cruelty-free brands include Hourglass (100% vegan, Leaping Bunny certified), Kosas (clean beauty focus), and Pat McGrath Labs (PETA certified). These brands prove that the highest levels of performance, artistry, and ingredient quality are fully achievable without animal testing.

Hourglass stands out by committing to being fully vegan by the end of its reformulation process. Their Vanish Seamless Finish Foundation, Ambient Lighting Powder, and Confession Lipstick are considered among the best products in their categories. Prices range from $30 to $79.

The Parent Company Problem

One of the most contentious issues in cruelty-free beauty is the parent company question. Several cruelty-free brands are owned by parent companies that are not cruelty-free. NYX and Urban Decay are owned by L'Oreal. Too Faced is owned by Estee Lauder. CoverGirl is owned by Coty. The debate centers on whether buying from a cruelty-free subsidiary financially supports a parent company that tests on animals.

There are valid arguments on both sides. Purchasing from cruelty-free subsidiaries signals market demand for ethical practices and incentivizes parent companies to move their other brands toward cruelty-free status. On the other hand, the profits ultimately flow to a company that has not committed to ending animal testing across all its brands. The strictest certification, Choose Cruelty Free (CCF), excludes brands owned by non-cruelty-free parents. Leaping Bunny and PETA certify the brand independently of its parent company.

The choice is personal. If parent company ethics matter to you, focus on independently owned cruelty-free brands like e.l.f., Rare Beauty, Milk Makeup, Tower 28, and Ilia, which are not owned by companies that test on animals.

China and Animal Testing

For decades, China required animal testing for all imported cosmetics, which meant any brand sold in mainland China could not be truly cruelty-free. This changed in 2021 when China eliminated the mandatory animal testing requirement for general cosmetics (like lipstick, foundation, and eyeshadow). However, special-use cosmetics (sunscreen, hair dye, whitening products) still require animal testing in China.

The regulatory shift has allowed more international brands to sell in China while maintaining cruelty-free status, but the situation remains complex. Brands may need to provide post-market safety data, and Chinese authorities retain the right to conduct animal testing on products pulled from store shelves for safety investigations. Brands truly committed to cruelty-free status are navigating these requirements through alternative testing data and careful product categorization.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does cruelty-free actually mean in makeup?
Cruelty-free means neither the finished product nor its ingredients were tested on animals. The term is not legally regulated, so look for third-party certifications: Leaping Bunny (gold standard with annual audits), PETA Beauty Without Bunnies, or Choose Cruelty Free. A brand can be cruelty-free without being vegan.
Is cruelty-free the same as vegan?
No. Cruelty-free means no animal testing. Vegan means no animal-derived ingredients like beeswax, carmine, lanolin, or collagen. A product can be cruelty-free but not vegan, and vice versa. For fully ethical makeup, look for products that are both cruelty-free certified and labeled vegan.
Can cruelty-free makeup perform as well as conventional brands?
Absolutely. Cruelty-free status has no bearing on performance. Fenty Beauty, Charlotte Tilbury, and Rare Beauty consistently match or outperform non-cruelty-free competitors. The market has matured to the point where there is zero performance compromise for going cruelty-free.
Why do some brands test on animals if alternatives exist?
Primarily to sell in markets like mainland China, which historically required animal testing for imported cosmetics. China relaxed these rules in 2021 for general cosmetics but still requires testing for special-use products. Some brands also use ingredients with limited non-animal safety data. The landscape is shifting toward alternatives.
What certifications should I look for?
Leaping Bunny is the gold standard with annual independent audits. PETA Beauty Without Bunnies is widely recognized. Choose Cruelty Free (CCF) is the strictest, excluding brands owned by non-cruelty-free parent companies. Verify on the certifying organization's website rather than trusting packaging alone.
Are drugstore cruelty-free brands as good as luxury ones?
Many drugstore cruelty-free brands deliver excellent performance. e.l.f., NYX, and Milani regularly rank alongside luxury brands in independent testing. The main differences at higher price points are packaging, shade range breadth, and ingredient purity rather than fundamental performance.
